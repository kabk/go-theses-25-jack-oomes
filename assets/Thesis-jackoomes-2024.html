Can Buddhist thought rectify my own personal shortcomings and biases as a Westerner?

 

 

Chapter 1 {What is Buddhism}

According to reports Siddhartha Gautama (est. 566BCE–486BCE) was born as a child of royal parents in Southern Nepal. Life was a treat in the palace of his upbringing, but life behind it’s walls weren’t part of his royal habituation, as he never set a foot outside of his home. This other side got him curious and the attractiveness coming along made him decide to leave everything he knew behind. Outside these walls he found out that the worldview his past had created was a soft version of the hard reality he got introduced to. This surprise was the beginning of his process to enlightenment. Dukkha or the suffering, he witnessed and felt became his reason to follow an ascetic way of living. Without any possessions he wandered, meditated and thought. Until it eventually brought him to the Bodhi Tree. Here, he turned from Siddhartha Gautama to the Buddha. Stranded underneath this tree, answers to his questions grew from his thoughts and formed a philosophy able to stop all human suffering[1].

 

In the upcoming centuries this philosophy spread across Asia. As time went by, different approaches emerged as different perspectives dealt with these ‘holy’ words. The further from the Buddha’s time on earth the more some of the new practices began to differentiate. Philosophy is open for interpretation, and is shaped by individuals. Every School has it’s own tradition, with it’s own unique take on Buddhism. But whether it’s Mahayana, Theravada, Vajrayana, Tantric, Chan, Zen or any other school, all are rooted from the same philosophy. This coherency in thinking — this root — is the starting point for a network of ideas. One that keeps expanding. Well known thinkers like Nagarjuna, Kamalashila and Miphan Rinpoche are famous for their own philosophies but all can be labelled as Buddhist thinkers. It’s hard to talk about Buddhism when there’s no such thing as one Buddhism. There is simply too much contrast in approaches. But the overarching term of Buddhism does have this starting point, coherency in the form of basic principles.

 

Suffering is often defined as inner, mental and emotional pain. Dissatisfaction, fear and shame for example. Traditional accounts of Buddhist ethics focus on the Noble Eightfold Path that leads to achievement of Nirvana, A state free of all suffering desire and full of satisfaction and insight. This Noble Eightfold Path exist of a process of attention points: ‘Right understanding’, ’right thought’, ‘right speech’, ‘right action’, ‘right livelihood’, ‘right effort’, ‘right mindfulness’, and ‘right concentration’. Three additional but fundamental prerequisites are abstention from harmful actions, a disciplined mind and a proper understanding of the self. On the other side, standing towards Nirvana, is Samsara. Which stands for a state of being in which ignorance leads the life of an individual and suffering is present. 

The concept of Nirvana and Samsara also defines the Buddhist perspective of death and afterlife. With Nirvana being the liberation from the cycle of death and rebirth, it’s counterpart Samsara stands for the endless cycle of death and rebirth. A process you want to escape from. Samsara means rebirth, stemming from the idea that life is cyclical. After death an individual is reborn in another body and the cycle continues. The form this rebirth takes on depends on an individual’s build up karma, someone’s moral action. Before the Buddha began sitting under the Bodhi Tree, he insisted on finding a way that ensures an escape from Samsara. The eventual insights that made him succeed and ‘awaken’ are called the ‘Four Noble Truths’. ‘The truth of suffering’: Suffering is an unavoidable part of life. When reaching Nirvana, you escape from life and so from suffering. Accepting life how it is, is essential in breaking from the cycle of suffering. ‘The truth of the origin of suffering’: Suffering originates from desire — wanting or wishing for something to happen or not to happen — a craving that originates within. ’The truth of cessation of suffering’: Ignorance about the nature of the self. This ignorance is a misconception in which people tend to assume that we are something absolute and independent. According to the Buddha the self is a mental construct. He even speaks of a non-self (Anatta). Something continuously changing, something that exists out of endless processes. Something that is connected to the rest of the world part of a whole rather than a fixed, independent entity. Therefore when one desires, one believes in fulfilling something that will never be absolute. When gaining this insight and removing ignorance one will be able to detach itself from desire. Finally, ’The truth of the Path’: The Noble Eightfold path, a guide that turns the past three insights to action[2]. 

Inseparable from practicing is meditation. This is can be viewed as the stepping stone to light, the most important research method. Buddhism doesn’t exist without. Trying let go of the ‘self’ by concentration, breathing to stay relaxed. A difficult process that takes time to master. Really understanding the Four Noble Truths needs study. We, humans are not programmed to assume these insights. We are programmed to look from our own perspective, that of the ‘self’. Buddhism teaches to never take anything for granted. Even your own perspective. Your own subjective truth. Different schools have different methods on tackling ones problems, but generally it’s believed that everyone has the potential to reach Nirvana.

These principles are the foundation, per school the approaches vary. Even in topics that can seem fundamental. Some honour the Buddha with reverence whilst other might worship him in a more expressive manner[3]. The Mahayana separates itself by developing a rich array of buddhas and boddhisattvas, one that progressed to the highest stages of Buddha-hood and that are able to act as if they are were gods[4]. According to Theravada Buddhism on the other end, the Buddha was nothing more than a human being that reached Nirvana. The Theravada and Mahayana are the two largest Buddhist schools. Two overarching approaches from which idea’s flow. The Mahayana in the north and the Theravada in the south, with North-east India as the centre.

The Mahayana, the Great Vehicle regards itself as the universal way to salvation that all should follow. This is obtained by dedicating oneself to the well-being of the world. Bodhisattvas are able to act like gods because they dedicate their lives to working to save others. This is their path to Nirvana. The further from the Buddha’s death the more he grew to be seen as supernatural. The Mahayana originated around estimated 100BC, approximately 200 years after the Theravada. Theravada or the Elder tradition, focusses more on the early and considered more traditional teachings of the Buddha. Different then Mahayanistic ideas about reaching Nirvana through serving the outer world, salvation is sought within. Emphasis on yourself versus emphasis on others[5]. Though for both, Buddhism will always be a personal research on ‘the self’ or ‘non-self’. How that is realised, depends.

The subjectiveness of this research makes Buddhism open for interpretation. Chances are low that a Buddhist will claim that it’s own study is the ultimate truth. Because of the absence of militant dogma’s in the base, many new approaches originated as the religion spread through Asia. Local traditions and believes merged when Buddhism arrived in the regional discourse. In China, Buddhism merged with Taoism and turned into Chan-Buddhism. In Japan, Chan-Buddhism merged with the local Japanese culture and turned to Zen-Buddhism.  The most well-known form in the West is Mindfulness, a blend between psychology and Buddhist meditation introduced by Jon Kabat-Zinn[6].
